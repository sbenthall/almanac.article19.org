1. The number of formal positions in standards developing organisations such as working group/committee chairs by actor

Formal positions have a direct impact on shaping the agenda and discussions, who has the chance to speak, and which proposals and contributions are accepted. From ARTICLE 19’s own experience engaging in technical standards, we’ve seen chairs have total control of when a meeting starts and ends, particularly when conducted remotely.

2. The number of authorships of published technical standards by actor

Authorship is one of the most direct ways to engage in standards development. Authors have more control over the process as they lead and facilitate the proposal through decision-making discussions. As technical standardisation is a lengthy process, a draft or a proposal made by a participant in an SDO must be reviewed and accepted by its technical community. Even if a draft is published, it is subject to iterative improvements and deliberations from the community. Publishing a draft does not guarantee that it will become a standard. By authoring a published standard, participants must successfully dedicate time and resources, and persuade the technical community within the respective decision making process. Therefore, for the purpose of this research, we focused our analysis on authorship of published standards only.

3. The amount of actor’s involvement and communication in technical decision-making processes

Various standards developing organisations continue to use mailing lists as a primary mode of participation and communication in working groups and committees. Actors can actively make contributions and object to various proposals. Analysing mailing lists provides further information into how dominant participants’ involvement and communication is within the decision making process.

About the data

For this research, we leveraged various web-scraping tools to analyse which actors dominate technical standardisation through three modes mentioned here. In order to study which actors dominate the development of technical standards, we use BigBang to provide a quantitative and qualitative analysis of mailing lists (mailman and Listserv).

How to use the dashboard?

Select the working group or organisation you want to look at. By default, you’ll see information about the “dnsop” working group in the IETF. The dashboard then contains three different visualisations for each authorship, influence, and leadership in the selected group or organisation.

On devices with a small screen information shown and user interaction is reduced; we therefore recommend using the dashboard on a screen with a higher resolution.

Hovering any of the three visualisation plots with the cursor will make available options for interacting with the plot appear on the top right. Here are the basic options:

  • Each plot can be zoomed in, zoomed out, and reset to the initial scale.
  • Exporting plots. Plots can be exported by clicking on the camera icon. The export will always show the current view (i.e the currently visible selection and current zoom level). The exported file is a scalable vector image with a transparent background.

On the plots themselves, each actor can be shown separately. Isolate the actor you’d like to see by double clicking on their name in the legend below / to the right of each plot. Toggle more actors on and off by clicking on them.
